WHO IMCI Handbook 2005

Integrated Management of Childhood Illnesses

For sick children age 2 months up to 5 years
➕ young infants age 1 week up to 2 months

IMCI Process & General Danger Signs

IMCI target age: 2 months to 5 years (plus young infants 1 wk–2 mo).
4 main symptoms: cough/difficult breathing, diarrhoea, fever, ear problem.
Case management steps: Assess → Classify (colour‑coded) → Identify treatment → Treat → Counsel mother → Follow‑up.

🔴 General Danger Signs (ANY one = VERY SEVERE)

  • Unable to drink or breastfeed
  • Vomits everything
  • Convulsions (fits)
  • Lethargic or unconscious

Colour‑coded triage

🟢 Green = home care
🟡 Yellow = treat at RHU / clinic
🔴 Pink = urgent hospital referral

If child has 1 pink + 1 yellow → treat as PINK (urgent referral). Exception: dehydration needs 2+ signs for severe.

💡 Chronic cough >30 days → refer for TB/asthma/whooping cough.

💧 Diarrhoea & Dehydration

SIGNS (2 or more)CLASSIFYTREATMENT
Lethargic/unconscious, sunken eyes, not able to drink, skin pinch very slowSEVERE DEHYDRATIONPlan C (IV or NG fluids), urgent refer if other severe
Restless/irritable, sunken eyes, drinks eagerly/thirsty, skin pinch slowSOME DEHYDRATIONPlan B (ORS over 4 hours), continue feeding
Not enough signs for some/severeNO DEHYDRATIONPlan A (home fluids), continue feeding
Persistent diarrhoea (≥14 days): if dehydration present → refer; if not → special feeding, follow-up 5 days.
Dysentery (blood in stool): antibiotic for Shigella 5 days, follow-up 2 days.

🌡️ Fever, Malaria Risk & Measles

High malaria risk → VERY SEVERE FEBRILE DISEASE or MALARIA
Low malaria risk → VERY SEVERE FEBRILE DISEASE, MALARIA (no other cause), or FEVER – MALARIA UNLIKELY
No malaria risk → VERY SEVERE FEBRILE DISEASE or FEVER – MALARIA UNLIKELY
⚠️ Stiff neck = VERY SEVERE FEBRILE DISEASE → urgent referral.
Measles: generalized rash + cough/runny nose/red eyes → give vitamin A (200,000 IU if ≥12 mo). Corneal clouding or deep mouth ulcers = SEVERE COMPLICATED MEASLES.

🍼 Sick Young Infant (1 week – 2 months)

Possible serious bacterial infection (PINK): convulsions, fast breathing (≥60/min), severe chest indrawing, nasal flaring, grunting, bulging fontanelle, pus draining ear, umbilical redness extending to skin, fever/hypothermia, many/severe pustules, lethargic/unconscious, less than normal movements → IM antibiotics, prevent low blood sugar, keep warm, URGENT refer.
Local bacterial infection (YELLOW): red umbilicus or skin pustules → oral antibiotic 5 days, treat local infection with gentian violet, follow-up 2 days.
Feeding assessment: if low weight, not well attached, <8 feeds/24h, other foods/drinks → counsel, follow-up in 2 days (feeding problem) or 14 days (low weight).

💉 Immunization & Feeding

EPI schedule: BCG at birth; DPT/OPV/HepB at 6,10,14 weeks; Measles at 9 months. No contraindications to immunising a sick child well enough to go home. Only true contraindications: BCG for AIDS; DPT2/3 if convulsions/shock within 3 days of previous dose; DPT for recurrent convulsions/active neurological disease.
Feeding: Exclusive breastfeeding 0-6 months; continue breastfeeding up to 2 years; complementary feeding from 6 months (3x/day if breastfed, 5x/day if not). Active feeding, adequate servings.

👩‍⚕️ PHN Role & Treatment

  • Assess danger signs, fast breathing, chest indrawing, stridor, dehydration, fever, ear, malnutrition, young infant signs.
  • Classify using colour‑coded tables (pink/yellow/green).
  • Give urgent pre‑referral treatments: 1st dose antibiotic (IM if cannot drink), quinine for very severe febrile disease, vitamin A, paracetamol, treat to prevent low blood sugar (breastmilk/sugar water).
  • Treat local infections: tetracycline eye ointment (size of rice grain), wicking ear, gentian violet (half‑strength for mouth/thrush, full‑strength for skin/umbilicus).
  • Teach mother: ORS (Plan A/B), continued feeding, when to return (not able to drink, becomes sicker, fever, fast breathing, blood in stool).
